The real numbers

We will try to describe the reals \((\mathbb{R})\) as an extension of the naturals \((\mathbb{N})\), and assume the properties of the latter. The extension is threefold.
The first extension may be called the completion of \(\mathbb{N}\) w.r.t. addition i.e. the minimum extension of \(\mathbb{N}\) to enable the solvability of all equations of the form \(x + m = n\) for \(m, n \in \mathbb{N}\). This extension is the set of integers \(\mathbb{Z}\). (The \(\mathbb{Z}\) is from Zahlen, German for numbers.)
The second extension may be called the completion of \(\mathbb{Z}\) w.r.t. multiplication i.e. the minimum extension of \(\mathbb{Z}\) to enable the solvability of all equations of the form \(bx = a\) for \(a \in \mathbb{Z}, b \in \mathbb{Z}\setminus\{0\}\). This extension is the set of rationals \(\mathbb{Q}\). (The \(\mathbb{Q}\) is from Quotient.)
The third extension involves the completion of \(\mathbb{Q}\) w.r.t. order i.e. the minimum extension of \(\mathbb{Q}\) necessary such that every nonempty subset (of the resulting set) bounded above has a least upper bound, or supremum. This resulting set is the set of reals \(\mathbb{R}\).
One may also extend \(\mathbb{N}\) to \(\mathbb{R}\) in other ways, including the following two \[ \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Q}^+ \to \mathbb{R}^+ \to \mathbb{R}, \\ \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Q}^+ \to \mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{R}. \]

The natural numbers

A partial order on a set \(S\) is a relation \(\preceq\) that is reflexive, antisymmetric and transitive. The pair \((S,\ \preceq)\) is called a partially ordered set, or poset.
A triple \((X,\ g,\ x_0)\), where \(X\) is a set, \(g\) is a unary operation in \(X\) i.e. a function on \(X\) into \(X\), and \(x_0\) is an element of \(X\) is a unary system.
A homomorphism between two unary systems \((X,\ s,\ x_0)\) and \((Y,\ t,\ y_0)\) is a mapping \(h : X \to Y\) such that, \[ h(x_0) = y_0 \ \text{, and} \\ h(s(x)) = t(h(x)). \] If \(h\) is bijective then it is an isomorphism and the two unary systems are said to be isomorphic.
An integral system is a unary system \((X,\ s,\ x_0)\) such that
  1. s is an injective mapping on \(X\) into \(X \setminus \{x_0\},\) and
  2. if \(Y\) is a subset of \(X\) such that \(x_0 \in Y\) and \(s(y) \in Y\ \forall y \in Y,\) then \(Y=X.\)
In case of natural numbers that we're used to from our school days, we have \[ X = \mathbb{N} = \{1,\ 2,\ 3,\ 4,\ ....\}, \\ x_0 = 1,\ \text{and} \\ s : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N} \setminus \{1\} \\ s(n) = n + 1. \] Also note that, for an integral system, i. and ii. \(\implies s\) is a surjective mapping onto \(X \setminus \{x_0\}.\)
Why ?
Consider the set \[ s(X) = \{y \in X\setminus\{x_0\} : y = s(x) \ \text{for some} \ x \in X\}. \] Due to \(x_0 \notin s(X)\) and \(s\) being injective with domain \(X\) (i.), for every \(y \in s(X)\) we have \(s(y) \in s(X)\). Thus by (ii.) we have \[ \{x_0\} \cup s(X) = X \\ \iff s(X) = X\setminus\{x_0\}. \]
Any two integral systems are isomorphic.
Toggle proof
The following proof is taken from [Stoll]. Let \((X,\ s,\ x_0)\) and \((Y,\ t,\ y_0)\) be two integral systems, then there exists a homomorphism \(f : X \to Y\) and a homomorphism \(g : Y \to X\). We contend that \(g \circ f = i_X\), the identity function on \(X\). Define \(Z\) as the set of all \(x \in X\) such that \((g \circ f)(x) = x\). By definition, \[ f(x_0) = y_0,\ f(s(x)) = t(f(x)) \ \forall x \in X, \ \text{and} \ g(y_0) = x_0,\ g(t(y)) = s(g(y)) \ \forall y \in Y. \\ \therefore (g \circ f)(x_0) = g(f(x_0)) = g(y_0) = x_0,\ and \\ (g \circ f)(s(x)) = g(f(s(x))) = g(t(f(x))) = s(g(f(x))) = s(x) \ \forall x \in Z \\ \text{i.e.} \ x \in Z \implies s(x) \in Z. \] Thus, \(Z = X\) and \(g \circ f = i_X\). Similarly, \(f \circ g = i_Y\), the identity function on \(Y\). Thus, we have shown that the two homomorphisms are indeed bijective and, in particular, mutual inverses; i.e. they are isomorphisms, and the systems are isomorphic.
The natural number system is defined as the set \(\mathbb{N}\) of natural numbers along with two binary operations \(+\) and \(\cdot\), and a relation \(\leq\) satisfying the following properties,
    1. \((\mathbb{N},\ \leq)\) is a well-ordered set, by which we mean that \((\mathbb{N},\ \leq)\) is a poset in which every nonempty subset of \(\mathbb{N}\) has a least element.
    2. \(x < y \ \text{and}\ y < z \implies x < z\ \forall x,\ y,\ z \in \mathbb{N}.\)
    3. (Law of trichotomy) \(\forall x,\ y \in \mathbb{N}\) exactly one of the following holds, \[ x = y,\ x < y,\ y < x. \]

Sequences

(Useful inequalities)
  1. \(\lvert x - a \rvert < \epsilon, \ \lvert y - a \rvert \leq \epsilon \\ \iff -\epsilon < x - a < \epsilon, \ -\epsilon \leq y - a \leq \epsilon \\ \iff a - \epsilon < x < a + \epsilon, \ a - \epsilon \leq y \leq a + \epsilon \\ \iff x \in (a - \epsilon,\ a + \epsilon), \ y \in [a - \epsilon,\ a + \epsilon]. \)
  2. \(\lvert x y \rvert = \lvert x \rvert \cdot \lvert y \rvert\).
  3. \(\lvert x + y \rvert \leq \lvert x \rvert + \lvert y \rvert\). (Triange Inequality.)
  4. \(\lvert \lvert x \rvert - \lvert y \rvert \rvert \leq \lvert x - y \rvert.\)
  5. \(\displaystyle (\sum_{i=1}^n a_{i}^2)(\sum_{i=1}^n b_{i}^2) \geq (\sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i)^2\). (Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality.)

Consider a point \(p \in \mathbb{R}.\) Then a neighbourhood of \(p\) is defined as, \[\displaystyle N(p; r) := \{x \in \mathbb{R} : \lvert x − p \rvert < r\} \\ = \{x : p − r < x − p < p + r\} \\ = (p−r,\ p+r). \]
