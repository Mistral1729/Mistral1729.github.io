Introduction

Prove or disprove: In any gathering of \(n > 1\) people, there are at least two people with exactly the same number of friends.
Toggle solution
It is true that in any gathering of \(n > 1\) people, there are at least two people with exactly the same number of friends.

Number of friends for a vertex \(v\) is \(f_v \in \{0, 1, ..., n-1\} = [n-1]\). If \(S\) is the set of \(f_v\)'s for all vertices \(v\) in the graph, then \(0 \in S \implies (n-1) \notin S\) and \((n-1) \in S \implies 0 \notin S\), i.e. both \(0\) and \(n-1\) can't occur in \(S\). Also, \[\displaystyle Card(\{[n-1]\setminus\{0\}\}) = Card(\{[n-1]\setminus\{n-1\}\}) \\ = n - 1 < n. \] Thus the statement follows from pigeonhole principle.
(Ramsey problem) Prove or disprove: In any gathering of \(6\) people, either \(3\) are mutually friends or \(3\) are mutually strangers.
Toggle solution
It is true that in any gathering of \(6\) people, either \(3\) are mutually friends or \(3\) are mutually strangers.
For any person \(u\), \(\text{Friends}(u) + \text{Strangers}(u) = 5\) clearly holds.
Thus, for any person \(u\) either \(\text{Friends}(u) \geq 3\) or \(\text{Strangers}(u) \geq 3\).
