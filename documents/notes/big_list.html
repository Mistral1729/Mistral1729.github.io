Introduction

From the 1992 Putnam ( [Klosinski]) we have the following problem,

A6.
Four points are chosen at random on the surface of a sphere. What is the probability that the center of the sphere lies inside the tetrahedron whose vertices are at the four points ? (It is understood that each point is independently chosen relative to a uniform distribution on the sphere.)
Solution
Recall that if four points are in general position in 3-space, then a point \(P\) lies in the tetrahedron formed by said four points iff the barycentric coordinates of \(P\) with respect to the tetrahedron's vertices are positive. Call the four points \(P_{0},\ P_{1},\ P_{2},\ P_{3}\). Then,
\(\displaystyle \vec{P} = \sum_{k=0}^{3}p_{k}\vec{P_{k}} \Rightarrow P\) is in the interior of \(P_{0}P_{1}P_{2}P_{3}\) iff \(p_k\) > 0.
Fix the center of the sphere as the origin, fix \(P_0\) on the sphere's north pole, with \(P_i\) chosen at random for \(i = 1, 2, 3\). Then the center is in the interior of \(P_{0}P_{1}P_{2}P_{3}\) iff there is a solution (\(p_{0}, p_{1}, p_{2}, p_{3}\)) to,
\(\displaystyle \vec{0} = \sum_{k=0}^{3}p_{k}\vec{P_{k}}\)
with the \(p_k\)'s having the same sign - also note that the solution space being 1-dimensional means that if the condition holds for a nonzero solution then it holds for all.
Each \(P_i\) is chosen in two steps,
  1. a random diameter \(Q_{i1}Q_{i2}\) is chosen, and
  2. the endpoints \(Q_{i1},\ Q_{i2}\) are chosen at random,
such that we have \(2^3 = 8\) possible selections of endpoints for the \(3\) diameters, each of which are equally likely so that each of the \(8\) tetrahedra \(P_{0}Q_{1j_1}Q_{2j_2}Q_{3j_3}\) with \(j_i = 1\) or \(2\) are equally likely. Thus, if \(\mathbf{E}\) is the set of all possible ways that four random points can be chosen on the sphere such that they form the vertices of a tetrahedron, then \(Card(\mathbf{E}) = 8\). For the tetrahedron to be degenerate all three points must coincide with \(P_0\) on the sphere's north pole. So, as \(\mathbb{P}(degeneracy) = 0\) clearly, we may suppose that the three points (the vertices of our tetrahedra) are in general position. Similarly we may suppose that the sphere's center doesn't lie on any of the faces of our tetrahedra.
Now let \((p_{0}, p_{1}, p_{2}, p_{3})\) be a nonzero solution to,
\(\displaystyle \vec{0} = p_{0}\vec{P_{0}} + \sum_{k=1}^{3}p_{k}\vec{Q_{k1}}\)
and as \(\vec{Q_{k1}} = -\vec{Q_{k2}}\) the \(8\) equations,
\(\displaystyle \vec{0} = p_{0}\vec{P_{0}} + \sum_{k=1}^{3}p_{k}\vec{Q_{kj_k}}\)
have the respective solutions,
\[\begin{align} \displaystyle \mathbf{S} = \{(p_{0}, p_{1}, p_{2}, p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, p_{1}, p_{2}, -p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, p_{1}, -p_{2}, p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, -p_{1}, p_{2}, p_{3}),\\ (p_{0}, p_{1}, -p_{2}, -p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, -p_{1}, -p_{2}, p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, -p_{1}, p_{2}, -p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, -p_{1}, -p_{2}, -p_{3})\}. \end{align}\]
In exactly one of the 8 solutions do the \(p_i\)'s have the same sign. Thus exactly one of the 8 equally likely tetrahedra contains the sphere's center (call it \(\mathbf{O}\)). Thus \(\mathbb{P}(P_{0}P_{1}P_{2}P_{3} \ni \mathbf{O} \mid \mathbf{E}) = \frac{1}{8}.\)

Fields

1.
Almost all the laws of elementary arithmetic are consequences of the axioms defining a field. Prove, in particular, that if \(\mathfrak{F}\) is a field, and if \(\alpha, \beta,\) and \(\gamma\) belong to \(\mathfrak{F}\), then the following relations hold.
  1. \(0 + \alpha = \alpha\).
  2. If \(\alpha + \beta = \alpha + \gamma\), then \(\beta = \gamma\).
  3. \(\alpha + (\beta - \alpha) = \beta\). \((\)Here \(\beta - \alpha = \beta + (-\alpha).)\)
  4. \(0 \cdot \alpha = \alpha \cdot 0 = 0\). \((\)For clarity or emphasis we sometimes use the dot to indicate multiplication.\()\)
  5. \((-1)\alpha = -\alpha.\)
  6. \((-\alpha)(-\beta) = \alpha \beta.\)
  7. If \(\alpha \beta = 0\), then either \(\alpha = 0\) or \(\beta = 0\) \((\)or both\()\).
2.
  1. Is the set of all positive integers a field ? (In familiar systems, such as the integers, we shall almost always use the ordinary operations of addition and multiplication. On the rare occasions when we depart from this convention, we shall give ample warning. As for « positive, » by that word we mean, here and elsewhere in this book, « greater than or equal to zero. » If 0 is to be excluded, we shall say « strictly positive. »)
  2. What about the set of all integers ?
  3. Can the answers to these questions be changed by re-defining addition or multiplication (or both) ?
3.
Let \(m\) be an integer, \(m \geq 2\), and let \(\mathbb{Z}_m\) be the set of all positive integers less than \(m\), \(\mathbb{Z}_m = \{0,\ 1,\ ...,\ m-1\}\). If \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are in \(\mathbb{Z}_m\), let \(\alpha + \beta\) be the least positive remainder obtained by dividing the (ordinary) sum of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) by \(m\), and, similarly, let \(\alpha \beta\) be the least positive remainder obtained by dividing the (ordinary) product of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) by \(m\). (Example: if \(m = 12\), then \(3 + 11 = 2\) and \(3 \cdot 11 = 9\).)
  1. Prove that \(\mathbb{Z}_m\) is a field if and only if \(m\) is a prime.
  2. What is \(-1\) in \(\mathbb{Z}_5\) ?
  3. What is \(\frac{1}{3}\) in \(\mathbb{Z}_7\) ?
4.
The example of \(\mathbb{Z}_p\) (where \(p\) is prime) shows that not quite all the laws of elementary arithmetic hold in fields; in \(\mathbb{Z}_2\), for instance, \(1 + 1 = 0\). Prove that if \(\mathfrak{F}\) is a field, them either the result of repeatedly adding 1 to itself is always different from 0, or else the first time that it is equal to 0 occurs when the number of summands is a prime. (The characteristic of the field \(\mathfrak{F}\) is defined to 0 in the first case and the crucial prime in the second.)
5.
Let \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\) be the set of all real numbers of the form \(\alpha + \beta \sqrt{2}\), where \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are rational.
  1. Is \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\) a field ?
  2. What if \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are required to be integers ?
6.
  1. Does the set of all polynomials with integer coefficients form a field ?
  2. What if the coefficients are allowed to be real numbers ?
7.
Let \(\mathfrak{F}\) be the set of all (ordered) pairs \((\alpha,\ \beta)\) of real numbers.
  1. If addition and multiplication are defined by \[ (\alpha,\ \beta) + (\gamma,\ \delta) = (\alpha + \gamma,\ \beta + \delta) \] and \[ (\alpha,\ \beta)(\gamma,\ \delta) = (\alpha\gamma,\ \beta\delta), \] does \(\mathfrak{F}\) become a field ?
  2. If addition and multiplication are defined by \[ (\alpha,\ \beta) + (\gamma,\ \delta) = (\alpha + \gamma,\ \beta + \delta) \] and \[ (\alpha,\ \beta)(\gamma,\ \delta) = (\alpha\gamma - \beta\delta,\ \alpha\delta + \beta\gamma), \] is \(\mathfrak{F}\) a field then ?
  3. What happens (in both the preceding cases) if we consider ordered pairs of complex numbers instead ?
