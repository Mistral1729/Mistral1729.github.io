Introduction : 1992 Putnam A6

From the 1992 Putnam ( [Klosinski]) we have the following problem,

A6.
Four points are chosen at random on the surface of a sphere. What is the probability that the center of the sphere lies inside the tetrahedron whose vertices are at the four points ? (It is understood that each point is independently chosen relative to a uniform distribution on the sphere.)
Solution
Recall that if four points are in general position in 3-space, then a point \(P\) lies in the tetrahedron formed by said four points iff the barycentric coordinates of \(P\) with respect to the tetrahedron's vertices are positive. Call the four points \(P_{0},\ P_{1},\ P_{2},\ P_{3}\). Then,
\(\displaystyle \vec{P} = \sum_{k=0}^{3}p_{k}\vec{P_{k}} \Rightarrow P\) is in the interior of \(P_{0}P_{1}P_{2}P_{3}\) iff \(p_k\) > 0.
Fix the center of the sphere as the origin, fix \(P_0\) on the sphere's north pole, with \(P_i\) chosen at random for \(i = 1, 2, 3\). Then the center is in the interior of \(P_{0}P_{1}P_{2}P_{3}\) iff there is a solution (\(p_{0}, p_{1}, p_{2}, p_{3}\)) to,
\(\displaystyle \vec{0} = \sum_{k=0}^{3}p_{k}\vec{P_{k}}\)
with the \(p_k\)'s having the same sign - also note that the solution space being 1-dimensional means that if the condition holds for a nonzero solution then it holds for all.
Each \(P_i\) is chosen in two steps,
  1. a random diameter \(Q_{i1}Q_{i2}\) is chosen, and
  2. the endpoints \(Q_{i1},\ Q_{i2}\) are chosen at random,
such that we have \(2^3 = 8\) possible selections of endpoints for the \(3\) diameters, each of which are equally likely so that each of the \(8\) tetrahedra \(P_{0}Q_{1j_1}Q_{2j_2}Q_{3j_3}\) with \(j_i = 1\) or \(2\) are equally likely. Thus, if \(\mathbf{E}\) is the set of all possible ways that four random points can be chosen on the sphere such that they form the vertices of a tetrahedron, then \(Card(\mathbf{E}) = 8\). For the tetrahedron to be degenerate all three points must coincide with \(P_0\) on the sphere's north pole. So, as \(\mathbb{P}(degeneracy) = 0\) clearly, we may suppose that the three points (the vertices of our tetrahedra) are in general position. Similarly we may suppose that the sphere's center doesn't lie on any of the faces of our tetrahedra.
Now let \((p_{0}, p_{1}, p_{2}, p_{3})\) be a nonzero solution to,
\(\displaystyle \vec{0} = p_{0}\vec{P_{0}} + \sum_{k=1}^{3}p_{k}\vec{Q_{k1}}\)
and as \(\vec{Q_{k1}} = -\vec{Q_{k2}}\) the \(8\) equations,
\(\displaystyle \vec{0} = p_{0}\vec{P_{0}} + \sum_{k=1}^{3}p_{k}\vec{Q_{kj_k}}\)
have the respective solutions,
\[\begin{align} \displaystyle \mathbf{S} = \{(p_{0}, p_{1}, p_{2}, p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, p_{1}, p_{2}, -p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, p_{1}, -p_{2}, p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, -p_{1}, p_{2}, p_{3}),\\ (p_{0}, p_{1}, -p_{2}, -p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, -p_{1}, -p_{2}, p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, -p_{1}, p_{2}, -p_{3}),\ (p_{0}, -p_{1}, -p_{2}, -p_{3})\}. \end{align}\]
In exactly one of the 8 solutions do the \(p_i\)'s have the same sign. Thus exactly one of the 8 equally likely tetrahedra contains the sphere's center (call it \(\mathbf{O}\)). Thus \(\mathbb{P}(P_{0}P_{1}P_{2}P_{3} \ni \mathbf{O} \mid \mathbf{E}) = \frac{1}{8}.\)
